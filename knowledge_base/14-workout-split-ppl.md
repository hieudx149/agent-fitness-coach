# Push/Pull/Legs (PPL) Split

## Overview
PPL divides training into three categories based on movement pattern:
- **Push**: Chest, shoulders, triceps (pressing movements)
- **Pull**: Back, biceps, rear delts (pulling movements)
- **Legs**: Quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves

## Schedule Options

### 6-Day PPL (Recommended for Intermediate+)
- Monday: Push
- Tuesday: Pull
- Wednesday: Legs
- Thursday: Push
- Friday: Pull
- Saturday: Legs
- Sunday: Rest

Each muscle group trained **twice per week** — optimal for hypertrophy.

### 3-Day PPL (Beginners or Busy Schedule)
- Monday: Push
- Wednesday: Pull
- Friday: Legs

Each muscle group trained once per week — suboptimal but better than nothing.

## Sample Push Day
1. Bench Press — 4x6-8
2. Overhead Press — 3x8-10
3. Incline Dumbbell Press — 3x10-12
4. Lateral Raise — 3x15-20
5. Tricep Pushdown — 3x12-15
6. Overhead Tricep Extension — 3x12-15

## Sample Pull Day
1. Barbell Row — 4x6-8
2. Pull-Ups — 3x8-12
3. Seated Cable Row — 3x10-12
4. Face Pull — 3x15-20
5. Barbell Curl — 3x10-12
6. Hammer Curl — 3x10-12

## Sample Leg Day
1. Squat — 4x6-8
2. Romanian Deadlift — 3x8-10
3. Leg Press — 3x10-12
4. Leg Curl — 3x10-12
5. Leg Extension — 3x12-15
6. Calf Raise — 4x12-15

## Who Is PPL For?
- Intermediate to advanced lifters who can train 6 days per week
- People who want high volume per muscle group
- Those who enjoy longer, focused sessions (60-90 minutes)

## Drawbacks
- Requires 6 days per week commitment for optimal results
- Can be fatiguing if recovery is suboptimal
- Less flexibility in scheduling
