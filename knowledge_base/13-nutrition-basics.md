# Nutrition Basics for Strength Training

## Macronutrients

### Protein
- **Function**: Muscle repair and growth
- **Recommended**: 1.6-2.2g per kg bodyweight per day
- **Sources**: Chicken, fish, eggs, beef, dairy, tofu, legumes, whey protein
- **Timing**: Spread across 3-5 meals for optimal muscle protein synthesis

### Carbohydrates
- **Function**: Primary energy source for high-intensity training
- **Recommended**: 3-7g per kg bodyweight depending on activity level
- **Sources**: Rice, oats, potatoes, fruits, bread, pasta
- **Timing**: Prioritize around workouts (pre and post) for performance

### Fats
- **Function**: Hormone production (including testosterone), joint health, vitamin absorption
- **Recommended**: 0.7-1.2g per kg bodyweight (minimum 20% of total calories)
- **Sources**: Olive oil, nuts, avocado, fatty fish, eggs

## Caloric Goals

### Muscle Building (Bulking)
- Caloric surplus of 200-500 calories above maintenance
- Aim for 0.25-0.5 kg weight gain per week
- Larger surplus = faster gain but more fat

### Fat Loss (Cutting)
- Caloric deficit of 300-500 calories below maintenance
- Aim for 0.5-1 kg weight loss per week
- Keep protein high (2.0-2.4g/kg) to preserve muscle
- Strength may decrease slightly — this is normal

### Maintenance (Recomposition)
- Eat at maintenance calories
- Possible to build muscle and lose fat simultaneously (especially beginners or returning trainees)
- Slower progress than dedicated bulk/cut cycles

## Hydration
- Minimum 2-3 liters water per day
- Add 500ml-1L for every hour of intense training
- Electrolytes matter for sessions longer than 60 minutes

## Supplements (Evidence-Based)
- **Creatine monohydrate**: 3-5g daily. Most researched supplement. Improves strength and power.
- **Whey protein**: Convenient way to hit protein targets. Not magic — whole food is equally effective.
- **Caffeine**: 3-6mg/kg 30 minutes before training for performance boost.
- **Vitamin D**: If deficient (common in indoor workers). 1000-2000 IU daily.
- Most other supplements have minimal evidence for effectiveness.
