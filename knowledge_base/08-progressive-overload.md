# Progressive Overload

## Definition
Progressive overload is the gradual increase of stress placed on the body during training. It is the single most important principle for continued strength and muscle gains. Without progressive overload, the body has no reason to adapt.

## Methods of Progressive Overload

### 1. Increase Weight
The most straightforward method. Add small increments (1-2.5 kg) when you can complete all prescribed sets and reps with good form.

### 2. Increase Reps
Keep the weight the same but perform more repetitions. Example: move from 80kg x 8 to 80kg x 10 before adding weight.

### 3. Increase Sets (Volume)
Add an additional set to an exercise. Example: progress from 3x10 to 4x10 over several weeks.

### 4. Increase Frequency
Train a muscle group more often per week. Example: bench press once per week → twice per week.

### 5. Decrease Rest Time
Perform the same work in less time. This increases training density and metabolic stress.

### 6. Improve Range of Motion
Perform exercises through a greater ROM. Example: deficit deadlifts, pause squats.

### 7. Improve Technique
Better form = more efficient force production = ability to handle more weight safely.

## Double Progression Method
A practical approach for most trainees:
1. Pick a rep range (e.g., 8-12 reps)
2. Start at the bottom of the range with a given weight
3. Each session, try to add reps
4. When you hit the top of the range for all sets, increase the weight and start at the bottom again

Example:
- Week 1: 60kg x 8, 8, 8
- Week 2: 60kg x 9, 9, 8
- Week 3: 60kg x 10, 10, 10
- Week 4: 62.5kg x 8, 8, 7 (restart)

## Rate of Progression
- **Beginners**: Can add weight almost every session (newbie gains)
- **Intermediate**: Weekly or biweekly progression
- **Advanced**: Monthly or per training block (mesocycle)

## Key Principle
If you are not progressively overloading over time, you are not growing. Track your workouts to ensure you are making progress. Even small increments add up significantly over months and years.
