# Training Guide for Beginners

## Starting Out
The first 6-12 months of training is the most productive period in your lifting career. Your body adapts rapidly to new stimulus. This is often called "newbie gains." Don't waste it with poor programming.

## Recommended Approach
1. **Start with a full-body program**, 3 days per week
2. **Focus on compound lifts**: Squat, bench press, deadlift, overhead press, barbell row
3. **Learn proper form** before chasing heavy weights
4. **Progress linearly**: Add 2.5kg to upper body lifts and 5kg to lower body lifts each session
5. **Keep it simple**: 3-4 exercises per session, 3 sets each

## Sample Beginner Program (3 days/week)

### Workout A
1. Squat — 3x5
2. Bench Press — 3x5
3. Barbell Row — 3x5

### Workout B
1. Squat — 3x5
2. Overhead Press — 3x5
3. Deadlift — 1x5

Alternate A/B each session: A-B-A, B-A-B, etc.

## How Long Does the Beginner Phase Last?
- Typically 3-9 months depending on consistency
- You are no longer a beginner when you can't add weight every session
- At that point, transition to an intermediate program with weekly progression

## Common Beginner Mistakes
1. **Program hopping**: Stick to one program for at least 12 weeks
2. **Too much volume**: Beginners don't need 20 sets per muscle group per week
3. **Neglecting form**: Ego lifting leads to injury and wasted time
4. **Not eating enough**: You can't build muscle without fuel
5. **Comparing to others**: Focus on YOUR progress week to week
6. **Skipping legs**: Don't be that person
7. **Not tracking workouts**: If you don't write it down, you can't ensure progressive overload

## Realistic Expectations (First Year)
- **Muscle gain**: 5-10 kg of lean muscle mass (males), 2.5-5 kg (females)
- **Strength gain**: Most lifters can double or triple their starting numbers on major lifts
- **Body composition**: Noticeable visual changes within 3-6 months with proper nutrition
- **Timeline**: Results come from consistency over months, not intensity over days
