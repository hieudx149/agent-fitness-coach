# RPE and RIR — Autoregulation in Training

## RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion)
A 1-10 scale measuring how hard a set felt. Originally from Borg's scale, adapted for resistance training by Mike Tuchscherer.

| RPE | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| 10 | Maximum effort. Could not do another rep. |
| 9.5 | Could maybe do 1 more rep but not confident |
| 9 | Could definitely do 1 more rep |
| 8.5 | Could do 1-2 more reps |
| 8 | Could do 2 more reps |
| 7 | Could do 3 more reps |
| 6 | Could do 4+ more reps (warm-up territory) |

## RIR (Reps in Reserve)
The inverse of RPE. How many reps you have "left in the tank."
- RPE 10 = 0 RIR
- RPE 9 = 1 RIR
- RPE 8 = 2 RIR
- RPE 7 = 3 RIR

## Why Use RPE/RIR?
- **Autoregulation**: Adjusts training to your daily readiness. Bad sleep? You might hit RPE 8 at a lighter weight. Great day? You go heavier.
- **Prevents overtraining**: Training to RPE 10 every session is unsustainable
- **Better long-term progress**: Most training should be RPE 7-8.5 (2-3 RIR)

## Practical Guidelines
- **Compound lifts**: Train at RPE 7-9 most sessions
- **Isolation exercises**: RPE 8-10 is acceptable (lower injury risk)
- **Beginners**: May struggle to gauge RPE accurately — this improves with experience
- **Deload week**: Keep everything at RPE 5-6

## Common Mistake
Beginners often underestimate RPE (they think RPE 8 but it was actually RPE 6). Recording weights, reps, and RPE in a training log helps calibrate over time.
